# Reading Comprehension Assessment Guide

## Purpose

This guide defines how to design and evaluate reading comprehension tasks for each CEFR level.
Use short, purpose-written texts (50–150 words) to keep the placement test fast and focused.

## Text Selection Principles

1. **Use familiar, everyday topics at A1–B1** — unfamiliar topics inflate difficulty artificially.
2. **Increase text density, not just length** — B2 texts use more subordinate clauses, passive voice, and abstract nouns.
3. **Topic suggestions by level:** daily life (A1), travel/work (A2), current events/relationships (B1), opinion/argument (B2).
4. **Keep texts under 120 words for A1–A2, under 200 words for B1–B2** in a placement context.

## Question Types by Level

| Level | Best Question Types |
|---|---|
| A1–A2 | Specific detail (Who? Where? When?), True/False, Main idea |
| B1 | Specific detail, Main idea, Simple inference ("Why did she...?") |
| B2 | Inference, Author's purpose, Word meaning in context, Text structure |

---

## A1 Reading Sample

**Text:**
> My name is Maria. I am from Brazil. I live in Toronto with my husband and two children.
> I work at a hospital. I am a nurse. I start work at 7 in the morning. I finish at 3 in the afternoon.
> After work, I go to the gym. I like exercise. At night, I cook dinner for my family.

**Questions:**
1. Where is Maria from? → Brazil
2. What is her job? → She is a nurse.
3. True or False: Maria works in a school. → False
4. What does she do after work? → She goes to the gym.

**What to look for:** Can the student locate specific facts in simple text? Understanding is almost complete if they answer 3 out of 4 correctly.

## A2 Reading Sample

**Text:**
> Last summer, Carlos went on a trip to Japan with his friend Pedro. They traveled by plane from São Paulo.
> The flight took 24 hours. When they arrived, they were very tired, but excited.
> They visited Tokyo, Kyoto, and Osaka. In Kyoto, they saw many beautiful temples. Carlos loved the food.
> He tried sushi, ramen, and tempura every day. Pedro didn't like seafood, so he ate rice and noodles.
> They stayed for two weeks. Carlos says it was the best holiday of his life.

**Questions:**
1. Where did Carlos and Pedro travel from? → São Paulo
2. How long was the flight? → 24 hours
3. Why didn't Pedro eat sushi? → Because he doesn't like seafood.
4. How long did they stay in Japan? → Two weeks
5. True or False: Carlos did not enjoy the food. → False

**What to look for:** Can the student find details and make simple cause-and-effect connections? Scoring 4/5 indicates solid A2 reading.

## B1 Reading Sample

**Text:**
> Remote work has become increasingly popular over the last few years. Many employees prefer working from home because they save time on commuting and have more flexibility. However, not everyone agrees that this is a positive change.
> Some managers argue that employees are less productive at home because there are more distractions. Others worry that remote workers feel isolated and miss the social aspect of office life.
> A recent survey found that 65% of workers preferred a hybrid model — some days in the office, some days at home. This suggests that the future of work will not be entirely remote or entirely office-based, but somewhere in between.

**Questions:**
1. According to the text, why do many employees prefer working from home? → They save commuting time and have more flexibility.
2. What are two arguments managers give against remote work? → Less productivity / more distractions; workers feel isolated.
3. What does the survey result suggest about the future of work? → It will be a hybrid model, not fully remote or fully office-based.
4. What does the word "hybrid" mean in this context? → A mix of two things / some days at home, some in the office.

**What to look for:** Can the student identify main ideas, retrieve specific arguments, and infer meaning from context? Answering 3/4 correctly indicates B1 reading comprehension.

## B2 Reading Sample

**Text:**
> Artificial intelligence is reshaping the way we approach creativity. Traditionally, activities such as writing, painting, and composing music were considered exclusively human endeavors. Today, AI systems can generate convincing stories, produce original artwork, and compose melodies in seconds.
> Proponents argue that AI serves as a powerful creative tool — one that amplifies human potential rather than replacing it. A writer, for example, might use AI to brainstorm ideas or overcome creative blocks. Critics, however, contend that AI-generated content lacks the emotional depth and intentionality that define genuine artistic expression.
> The debate is unlikely to be resolved soon. What is clear is that AI is already influencing creative industries, and professionals who fail to engage with these tools risk being left behind — regardless of where one stands on the philosophical question.

**Questions:**
1. What is the main argument of those who support AI in creative fields? → AI amplifies human creativity rather than replacing it.
2. What criticism do opponents of AI creativity raise? → AI lacks emotional depth and real intentionality.
3. What does the author imply by "professionals who fail to engage with these tools risk being left behind"? → That adopting AI tools is becoming necessary for professional relevance, regardless of personal opinion.
4. Find a word in the text that means "supporters" or "people who are in favor of something." → Proponents
5. What is the author's overall stance on the AI creativity debate? → Neutral / balanced — presents both sides without taking a clear position.

**What to look for:** Can the student distinguish main argument from detail, infer implied meaning, understand author's tone, and identify vocabulary in context? Answering 4/5 correctly indicates B2 reading.

## Scoring and Placement

| Reading Score | Placement Signal |
|---|---|
| Passes A1 only | Confirm A1 |
| Passes A1 + A2 | Confirm A2 |
| Passes A1–B1 | Confirm B1 |
| Passes A1–B2 | Confirm B2 or above |

## Notes for the AI Teacher

- Reading errors often reveal vocabulary gaps, not comprehension failure. If a student fails only vocabulary-heavy questions, note this for the vocabulary assessment.
- A student who self-reports "Advanced" but struggles with B1 inference questions is likely B1.
- Always give credit for correct answers expressed in different words than the text — meaning matters more than phrasing.
- Do not use these exact sample texts in production — they are reference examples. Generate new texts on similar topics using the same structure and word complexity.
